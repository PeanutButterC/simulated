Simulated Dataset - Analysis

Dylan Ebert

Terminology

  • Agent: The virtual agent, driven by a state machine. The agent consists of an invisible body walking around, and a spherical hand.
  • Target: A randomized sphere, cube, capsule, or cylinder that the agent is interacting with. The target can be held, on table or on ground, the three states that dictate what action the agent can take next.

Data Distribution

The dataset is driven by the following behaviors:

  1. Idle: The agent is not interacting with an object. In practice, this means that they have completed a previous action and are waiting for the target to stop moving, or are moving into position to execute the next action.
  2. Reach: The hand is moving toward an object in order to pick it up.
  3. Pick: The hand moves back toward the agent while holding the target.
  4. Put: The hand is moving toward a random position on the table while holding the target.
  5. Retract: The hand is moving toward the agent while not holding the target.
  6. Throw: The hand is moving rapidly in a random direction while holding the target.
  7. PrepareHit: The hand is moving toward a random position near the target, before executing the hit.
  8. Hit: The hand is moving rapidly toward the object from a random position near the target.
  9. PreparePush: The hand is moving into a random position near the target, before executing the push.
  10. Push: The hand is moving quickly toward and past the target's initial position.

These behaviors drive hand motion and interaction with the target, but don't account for everything happening in the data. They don't account for instantaneous actions like drop, where the target is detached from the hand. They also don't account for when the agent is navigating around the room, whether the object is held or not.

Let's see how these actions are distributed, on a frame-by-frame basis:

As may be expected, Idle is by far the most represented action. Throw and Hit are the least represented. There are two factors that account for this imbalance: 1) the frequency of actions and 2) the duration of actions. Next-behavior sampling probability is uniform, with the exception of the probability of Reach being doubled, to allow the target to enter the held state more often. Even so, some behaviors are executed less frequently because of their conditions. For example, Put is a lot less frequent than Reach, because it requires the agent to already be holding the target, at which point it may choose another action, i.e. Drop. Additionally, actions have different durations. Throw and Hit are very short.

Let's ignore duration, and see the frequency of each action:

Here, we see a much more consistent distribution. Reach > Pick, PrepareHit > Hit, and PreparePush > Push are uniformly sampled given an on table target state, with Reach > Pick having double the probability. Retract occurs after Put, Hit and Push, making it represented frequently. Put and Throw are underrepresented, because they depend on the target being in a held state.

There are two types of inputs: raw and quantized, and two types of models: direct and lstm. Since the lstm is meant to do the heavy-lifting of improving the representation, and quantization is meant to make this easier, we would expect the quantized, lstm-based classifier LSTMProbeQuantized to perform best. However, this isn't the case.

DirectProbeQuantized and LSTMProbeRaw are tied for best accuracy and f1-score. The direct quantized has a bit higher precision, while the lstm raw has a bit higher recall. In the case of raw inputs, the lstm helps the model learn better representations across the board. However, in the case of quantized inputs, the lstm actually hurts the model, yielding the lowest accuracy and f1-score. This is surprising, and demands deeper investigation.

Comparison

The confusion matrices are very similar for each input type, with the quantized model having errors slightly amplified. Some observations include:

  • Reach is sometimes predicted as PrepareHit and PreparePush. These involve the hand moving toward the target.
  • Retract is often predicted to be Pick, Throw, or Push. These involve the hand and object moving independently.
  • Reach is sometimes predicted as Pick, and vice-versa. Why?
  • PrepareHit is sometimes predicted as PreparePush, and vice-versa. This one can be expected, since they're very similar.

This table shows results from testing more models. All of these use a sampling rate of 1, or the original data framerate of 60fps, as this was found to yield better results across the board. When varying the discount factor between .8 and .99, a sweet spot of .85, or Higher Discount, did best. This could be due to randomness, but could be verified with more training runs.

Another model, CPC, or Contrastive Predictive Coding was also tested, which performed very poorly. I don't know if this is due to the model or due to bugs. The increased performance of shifted negative samples over the simpler zeros and random negative samples suggests that it's working as intended, but the poor performance fancy negative sampling method (using the most likely prediction from the next session) suggests that there's a bug. Most likely, there's a bug with fancy negative sampling, as well as other parameters/modeling decisions that could be adjusted to improve prerformance.

Better negative sampling and tuning may make CPC competitive, but this may not be necessary if the basic LSTM performs well on further evaluations. I think that it's best to move on with the basic LSTM for now, and take another look at CPC if the basic LSTM fails on future tasks. If not, CPC remains an option in future work.
